<h2>What Is a TDS Meter and How Does It Work?</h2>

<p>A TDS meter measures the total concentration of dissolved solids in water. It works by passing a small electrical current through the water and measuring its conductivity. The higher the TDS, the more dissolved solids are present. A <strong>TDS meter reading</strong> is expressed in parts per million (ppm).</p>

<p><strong>What TDS Measures:</strong></p>

<p><strong>Minerals:</strong> Calcium, magnesium, sodium, potassium.</p>
<p><strong>Salts:</strong> Chlorides, sulfates, bicarbonates.</p>
<p><strong>Metals:</strong> Iron, copper, lead (in higher concentrations).</p>
<p><strong>Organic matter:</strong> Some dissolved organic compounds.</p>

<p><strong>What TDS Does NOT Measure:</strong></p>

<p><strong>Bacteria or viruses:</strong> TDS meters cannot detect microorganisms.</p>
<p><strong>Heavy metals:</strong> While some metals contribute to TDS, the meter cannot distinguish between safe and harmful metals.</p>
<p><strong>Pesticides or chemicals:</strong> Organic chemicals may not affect TDS readings.</p>

<p><strong>Ideal TDS Levels for Drinking Water:</strong></p>

<p><strong>Below 300 ppm:</strong> Excellent water quality.</p>
<p><strong>300-500 ppm:</strong> Good water quality.</p>
<p><strong>500-900 ppm:</strong> Fair water quality.</p>
<p><strong>900-1200 ppm:</strong> Poor water quality.</p>
<p><strong>Above 1200 ppm:</strong> Unacceptable for drinking.</p>

<p><strong>Note:</strong> The BIS (Bureau of Indian Standards) acceptable limit for TDS in drinking water is 500 ppm, with a relaxation up to 2000 ppm in the absence of an alternative source.</p>

<h2>How to Use a TDS Meter Correctly</h2>

<p>Getting an accurate <strong>TDS meter reading</strong> requires proper technique:</p>

<p><strong>Step 1 – Calibrate the Meter:</strong> Most digital TDS meters come pre-calibrated, but it's good to check. Use a calibration solution or known TDS water to verify accuracy.</p>

<p><strong>Step 2 – Collect a Water Sample:</strong> Fill a clean glass with water from the source you want to test. For RO output, collect water directly from the RO faucet after the tank is full.</p>

<p><strong>Step 3 – Turn On the Meter:</strong> Press the power button and remove the protective cap.</p>

<p><strong>Step 4 – Immerse the Probe:</strong> Place the probe into the water sample, ensuring the electrodes are fully submerged but not touching the bottom of the glass.</p>

<p><strong>Step 5 – Wait for Stabilization:</strong> Wait 5-10 seconds until the reading stabilizes. The display will show the TDS in ppm.</p>

<p><strong>Step 6 – Record the Reading:</strong> Note the reading for comparison. For RO systems, you should test both the input water and the output water.</p>

<p><strong>Step 7 – Rinse and Store:</strong> Rinse the probe with clean water, dry it, and replace the protective cap.</p>

<h2>What Your TDS Meter Reading Tells You</h2>

<p>Understanding your <strong>TDS meter reading</strong> helps you monitor your RO system's health:</p>

<p><strong>Input TDS (Tap Water):</strong> This tells you the quality of your raw water. In East Godavari, input TDS often ranges from 500 to 2000+ ppm.</p>

<p><strong>Output TDS (RO Purified Water):</strong> This tells you how well your RO system is working. A properly functioning RO should reduce TDS by 90-95%.</p>

<p><strong>Rejection Rate:</strong> Calculate by subtracting output TDS from input TDS, then dividing by input TDS and multiplying by 100. For example: Input 800 ppm, Output 40 ppm. Rejection Rate = (800-40)/800 × 100 = 95%.</p>

<p><strong>Signs Your System Needs Service:</strong></p>

<p><strong>Rising output TDS:</strong> If your output TDS is increasing, the membrane may be failing.</p>
<p><strong>Low rejection rate:</strong> Below 90% indicates the membrane needs replacement.</p>
<p><strong>Little difference between input and output:</strong> The system is not working effectively.</p>
<p><strong>Sudden TDS spikes:</strong> Could indicate filter exhaustion or membrane damage.</p>
